Project Overview & Design Rationale

Dataset Introduction

Our visualization dives into the Chicago Crimes dataset, focusing primarily on a subset of the 50,000 most recent reported incidents. By focusing on the most recent data, we were able to provide a more contemporary look at urban safety patterns while ensuring that our web application remains performant and responsive for a high amount of data points. The data was sourced directly from the Chicago Police Department's CLEAR system via the City of Chicago Data Portal.

What This Visualization Reveals

As opposed to being just a tool for static statistical analysis, we chose to design this visualization more in terms of exploration. By using features like the zoom and temporal scatter plot, users can immediately spot trends such as the constant, daily density of common crimes like "Theft" and "Battery," as opposed to a more rare occurrence of "Arson" or "Homicide."

By utilizing the interactive brushing and its dynamic linked views, users can isolate any chaotic cluster of data points on the scatter plot and immediately translate that timeframe into a temporal breakdown bar chart.

Design Decisions & Interaction

We implemented interaction techniques from both Category I and Category II to make the visualization more interactive and engaging:

  • Visual Encoding: We chose a temporal scatter plot, which maps Date to the X-axis and Primary Crime Type to the Y-axis. This allows us to effectively highlight the frequency and density of particular crime categories over time.
  • Panning & Zooming: To handle the abundance of data points (we chose ~50,000), we implemented a scroll-to-zoom feature. This allows users to move from a month-long overview to a hyper-focused view of particular days or hours.
  • Selection & Highlighting: Initially, we added a pop-up tooltip effect for visual feedback, but we found that binding mouseover events to 50k data points added too much latency and clutter. We removed the tooltips and instead built this into the Brushing & Linking feature, where selected data points are highlighted and detailed in the dynamic table below the chart.
  • Annotation (Narrative Features): We added a vertical dashed line (crosshair) which follows the user's cursor. Our goal was to have this act as a guide to help users accurately align specific data points with the dates on the X-axis.
  • Brushing and Link-By-Queries: Users can click and drag over clusters of crimes to draw a custom filter rectangle directly on the scatter plot. All data points inside the drawn brush are dynamically selected and passed to the linked queries below the chart.
  • Dynamic Filters & Link-By-Queries: The active brush selection queries the dataset and passes it to two dynamic elements below the chart. First, a data table reveals the exact 'Date', 'Crime Type', and 'Arrest Status' for up to 100 of the brushed datapoints (capped to maintain performance). Second, a "Time of Day" breakdown bar chart calculates and translates the dense visual data into readable temporal statistics.

Development Process

The project was a collaborative effort. We split up the work evenly from the beginning. Abhijay handled Category I, while Aadhitya handled Category II.

Total Cumulative Time Spent: Approximately 15 hours.

Aadhitya: Perhaps the most time-consuming aspect was fine-tuning the D3.js zoom behavior to make sure that it keeps pace with a large dataset.
Abhijay: I found the most challenging part was implementing the brushing feature on top of the existing scatter plot. I ran into issues with performance, as well as clashing behavior between the brushing and left-click panning features. I ended up disabling the panning feature but keeping the zooming feature enabled to avoid any potential confusion.
I also had to implement custom features that would utilize the brushing, and found these to be challenging and novel since in this use case, the brushing is kind of pointless without the table and time of day bar chart.
